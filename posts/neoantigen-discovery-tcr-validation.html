Neoantigen Discovery and TCR Validation: Bridging Bench to Bedside

Neoantigens TCR Translational

The promise of truly personalized cancer immunotherapy rests on our ability to identify the unique mutations in each patient's tumor and harness the immune system to target them. Neoantigen discovery — identifying tumor-specific mutated peptides that can be recognized by T cells — sits at the heart of this approach. My work has focused on building and validating the pipelines that make this possible.

What Are Neoantigens?

Every tumor accumulates somatic mutations as it grows. Some of these mutations alter protein sequences in ways that create novel peptides — neoantigens — that are presented on the tumor cell surface by MHC molecules. Because these peptides are absent from normal tissue, the immune system can potentially recognize them as foreign, making them ideal targets for immunotherapy.

The challenge is that:

  • Not all mutations produce immunogenic neoantigens
  • MHC binding prediction alone is insufficient — we need functional validation
  • Each patient's neoantigen landscape is unique, requiring individualized pipelines

Building the Neoantigen Identification Pipeline

Our neoantigen identification pipeline integrates multiple data streams:

Step Approach Purpose
1. Mutation calling Whole-exome sequencing of tumor vs. normal Identify somatic mutations
2. HLA typing Patient-specific MHC class I/II determination Predict which peptides can be presented
3. Peptide prediction Computational MHC binding algorithms Prioritize candidate neoantigens
4. Functional validation T cell reactivity assays with patient PBMCs Confirm immunogenicity

Critically, we validated this pipeline using patient tumor samples — not just cell lines or model systems. This real-world validation is what distinguishes a research tool from a clinically actionable platform.

TCR Discovery Using Autologous B Cells

One of the most innovative aspects of our approach has been developing TCR discovery systems using autologous B cells as antigen-presenting cells. Rather than relying on artificial systems or dendritic cells (which are labor-intensive to generate), we leveraged the patient's own B cells to present candidate neoantigens to T cells.

This approach offers several advantages:

  • Physiological antigen presentation — B cells naturally express MHC class I and II molecules
  • Scalability — B cells can be expanded from peripheral blood
  • Paired TCR identification — Reactive T cells can be isolated for TCR alpha/beta sequencing
  • Functional validation — Direct confirmation that a TCR recognizes its cognate neoantigen

From Discovery to Therapy

Validated TCR sequences have therapeutic potential far beyond biomarker applications. They can be used to:

  • Engineer TCR-T cell therapies targeting patient-specific neoantigens
  • Monitor neoantigen-specific immune responses during treatment
  • Inform vaccine design by confirming which neoantigens elicit functional T cell responses
  • Track clonal dynamics of tumor-reactive T cells over time
The goal is not just to find neoantigens — it's to prove they matter immunologically, and to use that knowledge to build better therapies for each individual patient.

TLR7/8 Immunomodulator Vaccine Platform

Extending this work, I have been driving preclinical programs for a TLR7/8 immunomodulator-based vaccine platform toward first-in-human trials. By combining neoantigen targets with potent innate immune agonists, we aim to enhance both the breadth and magnitude of antitumor T cell responses — a critical requirement for durable clinical benefit.

The Path Ahead

As sequencing costs continue to fall and computational prediction tools improve, neoantigen-based immunotherapy is becoming increasingly practical. The pipelines and validation systems we've built provide a framework for translating genomic data into personalized treatments — moving us closer to a future where every cancer patient receives a therapy designed specifically for their tumor.
