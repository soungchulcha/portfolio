BAFF-R CAR T-Cell Therapy: Developing Novel Immune Monitoring Platforms

CAR-T Immunotherapy Single-Cell

CAR T-cell therapy has revolutionized the treatment of hematologic malignancies, but understanding what happens at the cellular and molecular level after infusion remains a critical frontier. At City of Hope, I serve as the primary immune-monitoring lead for the recently activated BAFF-R CAR T-cell Phase I clinical trial — a role that has pushed us to develop entirely new analytical platforms.

Why BAFF-R?

B-cell activating factor receptor (BAFF-R) is expressed on the surface of B-cell malignancies, making it an attractive target for CAR T-cell therapy. Unlike CD19-targeted approaches, BAFF-R offers a distinct targeting strategy that may complement existing therapies and potentially address resistance mechanisms seen with CD19-directed treatments.

Measuring CAR T-Cell Polyfunctionality

One of the core challenges in CAR T-cell immune monitoring is understanding not just whether the engineered cells persist in the patient, but how they function. A CAR T cell that produces only one cytokine is fundamentally different from one that simultaneously produces multiple effector molecules.

I optimized novel immune assays to analyze the polyfunctionality of CAR T cells infused into patients. This includes measuring:

  • Simultaneous production of multiple cytokines (IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-2, and others)
  • Degranulation markers indicating direct cytotoxic activity
  • Proliferative capacity upon re-encounter with target antigen
  • Exhaustion phenotyping to assess long-term functional potential

Single-Cell RNAseq of the Tumor Microenvironment

Perhaps the most exciting aspect of this work has been developing novel platforms for single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNAseq) of the tumor microenvironment before and after CAR T-cell treatment. This allows us to ask questions that were previously impossible:

  • How does the composition of immune cells within the tumor change after CAR T-cell infusion?
  • What transcriptional programs are activated in responding vs. non-responding patients?
  • Are there signatures of immune evasion emerging in the tumor that predict relapse?
  • How do bystander immune cells respond to the engineered cells?
Single-cell resolution is essential because the tumor microenvironment is not a monolith — it's an ecosystem of interacting cell types, and understanding therapy response requires understanding each component.

From Data to Clinical Decisions

The ultimate goal of immune monitoring is not data generation — it's translating biological observations into actionable clinical insights. By correlating our immune monitoring results with clinical outcomes, we aim to identify biomarkers that can:

  • Predict which patients are most likely to respond
  • Guide dose-escalation decisions in the Phase I setting
  • Inform the design of next-generation CAR constructs
  • Support rational combination strategies

Broader Implications

The platforms we're building for the BAFF-R trial aren't specific to a single target — they represent a generalizable immune-monitoring framework applicable across CAR T-cell programs. As the field continues to expand into solid tumors and novel targets, having robust, validated, and sensitive monitoring tools will be essential for understanding what drives durable remissions and what leads to failure.
